Tech Tools for the Cheater.

The news (third story down in NTK) that in Britain, you can remove the caller ID information off the last person to call your phone, seems like a smart idea that is long overdue.

I remember, when I worked for Comet, our manager was having an affair with the manager of a different branch.

Occassionally, on his days off, we would get calls to the store. “Hi, it’s Paul. I’m having a problem with my phone. Could you call me back to see if it’s working OK?” he would request. And we would comply. Until we realised that the reason he was doing this was to delete the Caller ID of his previous, let’s assume extra-maritial, caller and replace it with the store’s.

At which point, we started having fun. He’d call up and ask us to call back. We wouldn’t bother. And when he called back to ask why, we’d say “I just got a message saying the call couldn’t be connected. Looks like there is a problem with your phone.”

Second-hand Cell

Very odd state of affairs. I picked up a new cellphone today, to replace the unbearably godawful service I’ve been receiving from the gits at AT&T Wireless.

However, it appears that I am not the phone’s first owner. There were already calls in the call-log, and five or six numbers in the phonebook.

Using Google’s power and might, I was able to find that the number entered as “Home” belonged to a Professor of Economics at Yale University!

The moral of the story? Be very careful when returning merchandise to a store to which you have entered personal information!
