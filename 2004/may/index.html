➠ May 17, 2004

Spin Cycle

I’m a big fan of Bullshit!—The Penn & Teller bunk-debunking show. It’s a great show, which challenges the audience to be critical thinkers.

Sure, it’s easy to laugh at fundamentalist fuckfaces ruining the education for kids in Georgia, or sneer at the fools who believe lying bastards who pretend to talk to the dead. But what’s really fascinating is when they broadcast an episode investigating the real truth behind something you believe in.

The episode on Recycling was fantastic. They convincingly argued that there is no environmental gain to recycling paper (Do we need to recycle french fries in order to save potatos? No, we just grow more potatos. It’s the same with paper and trees), and there is no shortage of landfill space in America.

And then, to top it all off, they gave an incredible statistic: The New York Department of Sanitation estimates that 40% of all items that homeowners sort into recycle bins are not suitable quality to be recycled, so just end up in landfills anyway. Forty Percent! Cities have to pay for extra trucks and coloured bins, just to chuck trash onto the exact same heap as everything else that gets thrown out.

After half an hour of Penn & Teller (and about twenty minutes of Googling to check out their sources), I’m now convinced that our taxes are being wasted on efforts which aren’t helping the environment, and I want to do something about it. I’m going to start writing to my City Hall to find out how much it’s spending on recycling, and see where it goes from there. Should be interesting…

➠ May 4, 2004

Was our castle and our keep

I posted a photo earlier, but I figured I’d elucidate. Joy & I are now homeowners. Or, at least, mortgageholders.

We closed on Friday, then spent close to two hours on Saturday night at Home Depot—Joy getting paint, and me wandering round looking at random tools and deciding that I just had to own one. We’re painting this weekend, and hope to move in the weekend after.

The piece of advice that we wish we’d had time to listen to is “if you don’t trust your real estate broker, then find one you do trust”. Due to pressures of time, we were stuck with the guy who we happened to run into at an open house.

I suppose it was a bad sign that he immediately reminded me of Gil from The Simpsons. From the first meeting (when we’d told him that we were looking for properties in the $300–350k range, and he presented us with the option of buying a $800k property and splitting it with friends), to the day of the closing (when he proceeded to lose his temper on the phone—on three separate calls—to Joy, the seller and the lawyer representing the mortgage company), he was a king klutz who gave the impression that he’d never sold a house before, despite having 10 years of realtor experience.

He was unable to answer basic questions that we, as first-time housebuyers, had. He seemed bamboozled by the process of filling out forms like the Offer and Purchase & Sales Agreement. During the home inspection, he appeared more concerned with downplaying defects to save his commission than actually making sure the house was something we wanted to buy. Talking to him made me wish that telephones came with some way to bitchslap the person at the other end of the line.

Having to deal with Tom Bransten was the most stressful part of buying the house. Thankfully, we never have to speak to the man again. You are encouraged to avoid him too.
