Unsolicited Testimonial

This interview with Mark Fletcher of Bloglines is worth a read.

I just want to add a note of appreciation for Mark—Bloglines is pretty much a one-man-show at the moment, and Mark is wearing many hats: He’s architected a site from scratch that has scaled smoothly (and hugely) since its original release, programmed it all, and is sysadmining a pretty sizable chunk of hardware. At the same time, he’s working out the business and customer service side of things.

And he’s doing a bloody incredible job of it all!

I know of very few people that would manage to do just one of these tasks succesfully. Somehow, Mark is able to juggle them all. And from my email interactions with him, I’ve found him to be a thoroughly amiable and smart guy, who’s driven by building the features that help his users, not by petty personal issues and ego.

Mr WingedPig, I salute you!

➠ May 17, 2004

Spin Cycle

I’m a big fan of Bullshit!—The Penn & Teller bunk-debunking show. It’s a great show, which challenges the audience to be critical thinkers.

Sure, it’s easy to laugh at fundamentalist fuckfaces ruining the education for kids in Georgia, or sneer at the fools who believe lying bastards who pretend to talk to the dead. But what’s really fascinating is when they broadcast an episode investigating the real truth behind something you believe in.

The episode on Recycling was fantastic. They convincingly argued that there is no environmental gain to recycling paper (Do we need to recycle french fries in order to save potatos? No, we just grow more potatos. It’s the same with paper and trees), and there is no shortage of landfill space in America.

And then, to top it all off, they gave an incredible statistic: The New York Department of Sanitation estimates that 40% of all items that homeowners sort into recycle bins are not suitable quality to be recycled, so just end up in landfills anyway. Forty Percent! Cities have to pay for extra trucks and coloured bins, just to chuck trash onto the exact same heap as everything else that gets thrown out.

After half an hour of Penn & Teller (and about twenty minutes of Googling to check out their sources), I’m now convinced that our taxes are being wasted on efforts which aren’t helping the environment, and I want to do something about it. I’m going to start writing to my City Hall to find out how much it’s spending on recycling, and see where it goes from there. Should be interesting…
