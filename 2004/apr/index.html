Rumours of playfair’s Demise…

From the extreme FUD of “iTunes 4.5 won’t play back files you decrypted using playfair”, we now reach the honest truth:

iTunes 4.5 (and the accompanying iPod update) only fix the loophole that allowed playfair to get to your DRM keys. Once you extract the DRM key (using any unupgraded machine), you can still unlock the files.

If you have a PC running iTunes 4.2, or an un-updated iPod, you can still use VLC to extract the DRM keys. I purchased some tunes yesterday on my 4.5-running Mac, then copied them over to my PC, which still runs 4.2. It played them fine. After running VLC and getting the new drm key, I was able to copy it back to my Mac’s ~/.drms folder and run playfair to break the DRM. (DeCSSJon’s DeDRMS also works fine)

I now have, on my hard disk, unprotected copies of the files I purchased from the iTunes Music Store on Thursday 29th April using iTunes 4.5.

Sticklers’ Unite!

I’ve just found out that the thoroughly excellent Eats, Shoots & Leaves has now received its US publication.

I read the book upon its original UK publication, and loved it. It’s an ace combination of the history of punctuation (it never occured to me that at some point, someone had to invent the exclamation point), with guidelines on how to attempt to use it correctly—and where the controvery lies. (For example, I hate, despise and loathe the ”Oxford comma”, and refuse point-blank to use it.)

I’ve long been a grammar stickler—Indeed, when I started blogging again, my third post was obsessing about a semi-colon—and this book definitely helps me feel less alone. Want to find out how sticklery you are? Try the Punctuation Game; I’m a 92% stickler, apparently.

(Incidentally, the book’s title comes from a joke involving a panda bear entering a cafe, which is altogether cleaner than the original version of the joke that I know, where he visits a prostitute and it ain’t a gun that he shoots. I’ll let you all work that out for yourselves.)

Johnny One Note

I’ve long loved Microsoft OneNote. From its wobbly baby-steps during the Office 2003 beta program to the final-released copy, it’s Microsoft’s best new product in years.

If you’re unfamiliar, it’s an app which helps you organise unstructured/mildly structured notes. Perfect for taking notes during meetings, or capturing stuff off of the myriad of whiteboards I scrawl on on a daily basis.

The new OneNote SP1 Preview, released today, adds a bunch of new features, but none excite me quite so much as the ability to share out notepad pages across the network, and allow people to see what you’re writing, and make corrections in realtime.

Wiki-style networked virtual-whiteboarding with SubEthaEdit-esque editing in a Windows product? Sign me up now!

UPDATE: BTW, you don’t need to own a copy of OneNote to play with this new preview—Anyone can download, install and use for free until the end of August. Initial signs point to: It’s so fucking worth it!

Apple’s UI Sucks

One of the advertised features of Apple’s iSync package is that you can use it with their ($99-a-year) .mac service to access your Address Book on the web when you need it.

The other day, I was in a meeting and needed to get a phone number. I didn’t have my Palm Pilot in the room, but have no fear, I knew I could access it quickly via the .mac service. Here’s just how quick and easy it was:

  1. Visit http://www.mac.com/
  2. Click on the nice big “Address Book” graphic at the top of the page.
  3. Get taken to a promotional page advertising the wonders of the .mac address book.
  4. Hunt around page for link to Login. (Ignoring the “Logout” link on the toolbar which displays whether you’re logged in or not!)
  5. Eventually find small “Already a member?” link in middle of promotional text imploring me to join.
  6. Click it.
  7. Get asked for username & password.
  8. Enter username & password.
  9. Get taken back to the .mac homepage. Not the Address Book as I might have hoped.
  10. Hunt around for (smaller) Address Book graphic, now in a completely different part of the screen from before.
  11. Click it.
  12. Get asked again for password.
  13. Enter password again.
  14. Finally get to a screen where I can access my phone book.

I suspect that Apple’s famed UI team don’t speak to the website folks much.

➠ April 4, 2004

Playfair Building

When Apple’s comparitively-generous-but-still-restrictive policy for Music Store downloads was announced (summary: “you can play your music on 3 PCs and unlimited iPods”), I always figured the iPod was the loose link in the security chain. It either contained a skeleton master key to play any protected tune, or would store the user’s key in an easy-to-reverse-engineer spot.

And now, after far longer than I had expected, playfair (not, as far as I can tell, named after column-obsessed Scottish architect) tumbles into view, exploiting the loophole of the iPod (or, ironically enough, Windows PCs) to decrypt protected Apple music, and losslessly convert them to unprotected m4a files. Initial tests are successful.

Hurrah! I now own the music I paid for!

➠ April 2, 2004

The G stands for Value

I’ve been musing on what a fucking genius idea GMail is, so I thought I’d post an email I sent to the (still-not-webarchived) uk-netmarketing

Robbie Lockie wrote:

>> How is it logistical / technically possible to provide potentially over
>> 2 million users, with 1GB of space each!!!

Technically: very easy. Just buy lots of disks.

Logistically: Not everyone will use 1Gb. Hell, it’s damned hard to get
close! Especially if they limit the size of attachments.

I’m a pretty heavy email user, and I only get about 30–40Mb of mail a
month, so a gig of Google’s diskspace will give me a 2.5 year
repository, even if I never delete anything.

(Spam adds between 60 and 100Mb every month, so a decent spam filter
will be important to them.)

The real genius of GMail is that mailbox quotas are a MAJOR pain-point
for the users of Hotmail and Yahoo Mail. Some idiot friend mails you
you a hilarious 2 Mb WAV file, and suddenly everyone’s mail to you is
bouncing until you next get to a PC. So people are going to be clawing
at Google’s doors to get accounts.

And if Google manages to wow you with great search, filtering and
threading tools once you’re there, you’re going to be a great
word-of-mouther.

Excellent blogentry on using GMail, and reaction to the personalized
ads: http://www.phoenixfeather.net/archives/000035.html

Rod.

➠ April 1, 2004

Putting my “reputation” on the line

Dan thinks that the just-announced Google Gmail service is an April Fool. I’m willing to be money that it’s not for a variety of reasons:

  1. It was put out on BusinessWire, AP, etc, and published in places like CNET and NYTimes. Nothing pisses off the media quite like (unknowingly) publishing a hoax.
  2. The Privacy Policy looks thoroughly legit to me. Just covering their posteriors about mailing service announcements, and displaying related ads.
  3. The Moon Base is a typical Google April Fools joke. Link on the homepage and silly “news” story. GMail, not so much.
  4. The MX entries for gmail.com shows different (non-functioning) SMTP servers than the MX entries for google.com.

I could end up with egg on my face for stating this publicly, but I reckon it’s totally legit.

UPDATE: Vindicated.
