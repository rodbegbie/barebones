➠ March 29, 2004

Get in line to get in line…

… then take a number and wait to be called.

Anyone who thinks they’ve experienced bureacracy should try hanging out at the Immigration office.

There were plenty of tired and huddled masses around, but that’s because we were standing outside at 7am in the wind-tunnel-like environment of Government Center in Boston.

It was fascinating, listening to the stories of the other people in line. The words “since 9/11” were bandied around, as if a valid reason, by all kinds of people being there. The person behind me in the first line was a 70-year-old woman who’s held a Green Card for 40 years, but now has to renew it because of the changes since 9/11. Excellent work there, Dept of Homeland Security. We’ll flush those bastards out somehow.

After 30 minutes in line outside prior to the X-Ray machine, then another 30 minutes lined up in the lobby, I get to a human being. He takes my envelopes of paperwork, and without looking at them, sticks them inside a larger envelope. I don’t get a receipt. I have no kind of proof that I’ve submitted this huge wad of documents that determine whether I’m allowed to remain in the country or not. I can hope to receive one “within about 45 days”.

Hooray for Bush’s pledge to make the Immigration Services more efficient.

Usability != Security

I had an odd revelation this week.

For months, I’ve been praising a feature on Fleet’s ATMs called “Fleet Cash”. You tell it how much money you usually want to take out (say $80) and whether you want a receipt. Then, whenever you use an ATM, you just enter your PIN and hit the “Fleet Cash” button, and it dispense the money and returns your card immediately. It really speeds up the withdrawal process, and I’ve been raving about it to anyone who’ll listen since it was introduced. “Why doesn’t everyone do this?”, thought I.

However, while reading over my bank statement today, I realised that it’s a rather major security risk.

When I read my statement, I scan it, making sure nothing untoward catches my eye. If someone stole my account details and PIN (as is becoming increasingly common these days), and withdrew an unusual sum of cash (either too high, or too low), it would be easy to spot.

However, if they chose to just occassionaly make withdrawals of $80, there’s a greater chance of them getting away with it for longer—Especially on joint accounts. So programming the ATM with “Here’s the amount I usually withdraw” now strikes me as A Bad Thing.

And I’ll be curious to see how long the feature lasts. Time to read up on Fleet’s rules on liability for ATM fraud…

➠ March 17, 2004

Excel-lent

When you’re holding Excel, everything looks like a spreadsheet—A great post by Yoz comparing Excel to the Unix command line.

I absolutely agree. In my programming career, I’ve seen some incredibly whacked-out uses for Excel: Loading test data for systems, creating vast sprawling directory structures for reports, and (my personal favourite) a giant matrix defining screen-level security for an application, reading in Java code and spitting out SQL.

Sure you could use shell scripts or Perl to do all these things, but it would be considerably less pretty and far scarier to the non-Unix set.

I love Excel dearly. It’s by far the best thing Microsoft have ever done.

Routeword

I’ve been meaning to post about RouteWord for a while now. It’s a fun daily word puzzle—Follow the letters around the patch to make a word. My reason for posting this today? I created today’s puzzle.

That said, what really appeals to me about the puzzle is its origin as a tool for graph visualization. Takes me back to my Uni days looking at Turing machines and finite-state automata.

The submission applet is rather ace. Plug in words and see them turned into Routewords (Also fun: try entering palindromes like “madamimadam” or “amanaplanacanalpanama”). It’s strangely hypnotic.
