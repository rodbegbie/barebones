The Road Less Traveled

Just spotted this on Amazon. If you pre-order The Slippery Slope(Amazon.com: Books: The Slippery Slope (A Series of Unfortunate Events, Book 10)) now, you get to download the first chapter as a PDF. I immediately shoved my $9 into the sweaty fist of Mr Bezos. And here, for your delectation, is the opening paragraph.

A man of my acquaintance once wrote a poem called “The Road Less Traveled,” describing a journey he took through the woods along a path most travelers never used. The poet found that the road less traveled was peaceful but quite lonely, and he was probably a bit nervous as he went along, because if anything happened on the road less traveled, the other travelers would be on the road more frequently traveled and so couldn’t hear him as he cried for help. Sure enough, that poet is now dead.

➠ August 24, 2003

Pure Dead Brilliant

Greg Dyke announced today that the BBC could open up their archive to the internet “in the future”. This is a potentially incredibly exciting venture—Imagine being able to access any of the BBC‘s programming—Instead of just searching the internet to find the name of that sitcom where Elaine C Smith kept saying ‘Pure Dead Brilliant’(City Lights (1987)), you’ll be able to view an episode and remind yourself how dire it really was.

Being the BBC, there’s about a 75% chance they’ll do this “right”. However, knowing my luck, it’ll be UK-only like their BBCi Broadband service. Suddenly, all those open proxies on the UK networks might start getting used for more than just spamming.

Update: Danny O’Brien explains in much better terms why this is so exciting. That’s why he’s the professional journalist, and I’m just a geek with a TEXTAREA to type into.

➠ August 14, 2003

St. Christopher Hates Me

At this precise moment in time, Joy & I should be somewhere close to Albany, NY. However, we’re not. We’re in our apartment.

Due to the power outage, all buses through New York State were cancelled this evening. Apparently, these new-fangled bus contraptions don’t function without mains electricity. Or something.

So, for the second time this year, our mapped-out-months-in-advance travel-plans have been fucked by the proverbial “acts of god”. Back in February, we were flying to NYC for a weekend. However, the snow had other plans. Cue an hilarious Planes, Trains and Automobiles style jaunt from Logan Airport to South Station to take a train, followed by us completely failing to make it to New York in time to use our expensive tickets to ‘The Producers’.

Tomorrow, we’re renting a car and heading north. I’ll be driving. For pretty much the first time in five years. For pretty much the first time in this country. For pretty much the longest road-trip I’ve undertaken in my life.

Wish us luck.

(Note: oh, and as I type, our DSL connection is dead, so I can’t fact-check that “St. Christopher” is, as I seem to recall, the patron saint of travelers. If I got it wrong, you can officially bite me.)

Thunderbird(s) are “Go”

I’ve spend the weekend playing with Thunderbird. It’s only at version 0.1, but it’s nice. Looks likely to become my mailreader of choice very, very quickly.

I’ve been perfectly happy using Microsoft Outlook Express for the last few years, especially since OE-Quotefix made its output readable. But the problem with OE was that I knew that there was likely to be no upgrades in the forseeable future, and it’s a bloody nightmare for third parties to extend. Thunderbird is thoroughly open. Extensions are already available that handle GnuPG and tagline randomising.

Thunderbird isn’t anywhere ready to be recommended to everyone (you require some hard-ass geek confidence to make minor UI changes), but it’s definitely on its way there. Hurrah.
