161 Subscriptions and Counting

I haven’t written about Bloglines in a while, so it’s time for a reminder…

If you currently check a lot of websites everyday by hammering on your Favorites folder, you need Bloglines. The name is deceptive—It’s not just blogs you can track. Most anything online that updates frequently can be collated for your viewing pleasure, from the BBC News to BitTorrent warez.

I’ve been using Bloglines since, literally, Day One (back in the days when Dave Winer was going on about how great his entirely useless and forgotten subscriptions harmonizer was), and it’s fantastic to see all the improvements Mark has made. Being able to subscribe to searches is particularly great—I loved reading today all the theories out there in Blogistan about Rupert getting booted from Survivor. It can also be used to manage your blogroll—That list of “blogs I read” on the right of this page is generated automatically, based on my Bloglines subscriptions.

Go! Play! Subscribe! And become an infojunkie like me.

Security Blanket

Tog writes a great article on security usabillity.

I work in the offices of a Large Financial Institution. There are huge numbers of rules & regulations in place, which give some CxO the ability to check off a box on some list somewhere, that says that everything is “secure”. Passwords must be changed once a month, mustn’t be reused within a year, must have numbers, symbols, etc. The building is full of Post-It notes, natch.

But perhaps my favourite is that the Domain Administrator password is a Well Guarded Secret™. Only one person knows it. In addition, the servers are locked behind a door in the basement, which requires a sysadmin’s keycard to unlock. But it’s a pain in the arse having to trek up and down the stairs to go to the serverroom so the there’s an unlocked PC upstairs which is constantly running PCAnywhere, and connected to the Domain Controller. So anyone who is aware of, say, lOphtCrack can have full access to the network.

I think the key is that humans will always route around stupid, exploitable security, not out of malice, but laziness. Which just makes it easier for those who wish to affect malice.
